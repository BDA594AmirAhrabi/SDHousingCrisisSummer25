The Green Revitalization Imperative

In Downtown San Diego, a crisis of homelessness intersects with environmental injustice. This project explores how we can create sustainable, green public spaces that serve our most vulnerable residents, not displace them.

Case Study: San Diego's Dual Crisis

Understanding the complex dynamics of homelessness and urban development in Downtown San Diego is the first step toward finding equitable solutions.

What: A Tale of Two Cities

The core issue is a single, interconnected crisis: environmental degradation and social inequality. Affluent revitalization exists alongside a massive homelessness emergency, where poor air quality and urban heat islands disproportionately harm unsheltered individuals.

Where: Downtown's Contested Spaces

This crisis is concentrated in Downtown San Diego, particularly in neighborhoods like the East Village. This area is simultaneously a hub for social services and a hotspot for luxury development, creating intense gentrification pressure.

Who: The Vulnerable vs. The Powerful

The vulnerable group is San Diego's unhoused populationâ€”disproportionately people of color and those with disabilities. They exist in a landscape shaped by powerful stakeholders, including developers and city officials, whose goals often prioritize economic growth over social equity.

Why: A History of Exclusion

Decades of top-down, capital-led redevelopment have created a "revitalization" that displaces the poor. The narrative of "curing blight" has consistently led to policies and urban designs that exclude, rather than include, the most marginalized residents.

How: Ecogentrification & Hostile Design

The mechanisms of exclusion are clear. "Greening" projects raise property values, leading to **ecogentrification**. At the street level, **hostile architecture** (like benches with dividers) makes public spaces uninhabitable for those who rely on them for survival.

Insight: The Lived Experience

For unsheltered individuals, public space is not for recreation; it's for survival. The daily reality is a relentless search for safety, clean water, and a place to rest. Hostile design is not just an inconvenience; it's a constant message of dehumanization.

Data & Analysis

My research combined spatial analysis of displacement risk with a review of city policy and ethnographic studies to understand the problem from multiple angles.

Key Finding: Hostile vs. Humane Design

The design of public space is a choice with life-or-death consequences. We must shift from exclusionary designs to inclusive ones.

Feature Hostile / Exclusionary Approach Inclusive / Humane Approach
Seating Benches with dividers; sloped surfaces. Long, flat benches; movable chairs.
Shelter Open, exposed plazas with no cover. Covered pavilions; shade trees.
Water Decorative fountains; no drinking water. Accessible drinking fountains.
Restrooms No public restrooms, or locked facilities. Clean, safe, 24/7 public restrooms.
Storage No options for personal belongings. Secure, accessible personal lockers.
